If you've looked at any modern circuit board, you've seen two very different kinds of components: ones with legs poking through holes, and tiny ones sitting flat on the surface. These represent the two families of soldering — and knowing the difference shapes which kit and tools you should buy.

## Through-hole (THT)

Through-hole technology uses components with wire leads that pass *through* holes in the board and are soldered on the other side.

**Pros for learners:**

- Larger, easier to handle
- Forgiving — there's room for error
- The mechanical grip of the leads holds parts in place while you solder
- Perfect for understanding what a good joint looks and feels like

Almost every beginner [practice kit](/best-soldering-practice-kits) and project kit is through-hole for exactly these reasons. The [Elenco SP-1A](/reviews/elenco-practical-soldering-project-kit) is a textbook example.

## Surface-mount (SMD / SMT)

Surface-mount devices (SMD), assembled using surface-mount technology (SMT), sit directly on pads on the board's surface. They're how nearly all modern electronics are built because they're smaller and cheaper to manufacture.

**What's different for the solderer:**

- Parts are small — sometimes the size of a grain of rice or less
- You hold the part with tweezers while you tack one pad, then solder the rest
- Flux matters much more
- A fine tip and a magnifier or microscope help enormously

It's a real step up, which is why dedicated [SMD/SMT practice boards](/reviews/gikfun-smd-smt-welding-practice-board-ek7028) exist. They give you rows of footprints to practice on cheaply.

## Which should you learn first?

**Through-hole, without question.** It teaches the fundamentals — heat the joint, feed the solder, watch it flow — with components you can actually see and grip. Build a couple of through-hole kits until clean joints feel automatic.

Then graduate to surface-mount. Buy an inexpensive board (a [multi-pack](/reviews/dzrcoxi-smd-smt-practice-kit-3-pack) gives you several attempts), add a fine tip, good flux and tweezers, and practice placing and reflowing the smallest parts you can.

## Tools that bridge the gap

To move into SMD work you'll want:

- A **fine conical or small chisel tip** for your iron
- **Liquid or gel flux** — it makes solder flow exactly where you want
- **Fine-tip tweezers** for placing parts
- **A magnifier or USB microscope** — your eyes will thank you
- **Thin solder** (0.5 mm or finer)

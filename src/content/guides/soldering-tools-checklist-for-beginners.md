You don't need a professional bench to start soldering, but a few key tools make the difference between joy and frustration. Here's exactly what to buy, split into essentials and nice-to-haves.

## The essentials

These you genuinely need:

- **A temperature-controlled soldering iron or station.** This is the most important purchase. Stable, adjustable heat makes clean joints far easier. The [YIHUA 926 III station](/reviews/yihua-926-iii-soldering-station) is our top pick because it bundles tips and accessories; for portability, a cordless iron like the [Fanttik T1 Max](/reviews/fanttik-t1-max-cordless-soldering-iron) is excellent.
- **Thin rosin-core solder** (0.6–0.8 mm). Rosin-core means flux is built in. See our [lead-free vs leaded guide](/guides/lead-free-vs-leaded-solder) to choose.
- **A soldering iron stand.** Never rest a hot iron on the bench. Most stations include one.
- **Brass-wool tip cleaner.** Better than a wet sponge — see [tip care](/guides/how-to-clean-and-tin-a-soldering-iron-tip).
- **Safety glasses.** Solder and flux can spit.
- **Something to practice on.** A [practice kit](/best-soldering-practice-kits) gives you a safe, rewarding first build instead of risking a real board.

## The nice-to-haves

These aren't mandatory on day one, but each makes life easier:

- **Helping hands / a third-hand tool or PCB vise** — holds work steady so both your hands are free.
- **Flux** (paste or pen) — helps solder flow, essential for surface-mount and tricky joints.
- **Flush cutters** — for trimming component leads.
- **Solder sucker or desoldering braid** — for fixing mistakes (see [how to desolder](/guides/how-to-desolder)).
- **Fume extractor or a small fan** — to pull flux smoke away from your face.
- **A magnifier or USB microscope** — invaluable once you move to [surface-mount work](/guides/through-hole-vs-surface-mount-soldering).
- **Isopropyl alcohol + brush** — to clean flux residue off finished boards.

## The smart way to buy

Many beginners overspend on the iron and forget the consumables, or buy a cheap iron and immediately struggle. Two tips:

1. **Buy a station kit** that bundles tips, a stand, helping hands and solder — it's usually better value than buying each piece separately.
2. **Get a practice kit at the same time** so you have something to learn on from day one.

Once your kit arrives, set it up safely and follow our [step-by-step beginner guide](/guides/how-to-solder-for-beginners). You'll be making clean joints the same afternoon.

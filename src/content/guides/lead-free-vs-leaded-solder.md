One of the first choices a new solderer faces is which solder to buy. The short answer: **for learning, leaded is easier; for safety and compliance, lead-free wins.** Here's how to decide.

## What's the difference?

- **Leaded solder** is usually a 60/40 or 63/37 tin-lead alloy. It melts at a low temperature, flows beautifully, and forms bright, shiny joints — which also makes it easy to *see* when a joint is good.
- **Lead-free solder** is typically a SAC alloy (tin, silver, copper). It melts hotter, flows less eagerly, and tends to leave a duller, grainier-looking joint even when it's perfectly sound.

## Melting point and ease of use

| | Leaded (60/40) | Lead-free (SAC) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Melting point | ~188°C (370°F) | ~217–227°C (423–441°F) |
| Soldering temp | 315–340°C (600–650°F) | 350–370°C (660–700°F) |
| Flow / wetting | Excellent | Good, needs more flux |
| Joint appearance | Shiny | Often dull (normal) |
| Ease for beginners | Easier | Harder |

Because lead-free runs hotter and wets less, it punishes a weak technique more. That's the main reason it's harder to learn on. A good [temperature-controlled station](/best-soldering-irons) helps a lot with lead-free.

## Safety and regulations

Lead is toxic, so the obvious advantage of lead-free is **no lead exposure** — relevant for children, food-contact items, and products sold commercially. The **RoHS** directive requires lead-free solder in most consumer electronics sold in the EU and many other markets.

That said, *both* solders release flux fumes when heated. Those fumes — not the lead — are what you're breathing at the bench, so **ventilation and washing your hands after soldering** matter no matter which you choose.

## So which should you buy?

- **Learning at home, by yourself?** Leaded 60/40 (0.6–0.8 mm) is the gentlest way to build technique. Wash your hands and ventilate.
- **Soldering with or for kids, or building something others will handle?** Choose lead-free.
- **Building products to sell, or in a RoHS region?** Lead-free is required.

Whatever you pick, get **thin, rosin-core** solder so flux is built in, and pair it with the right [iron](/guides/how-to-choose-a-soldering-iron) and a [practice kit](/best-soldering-practice-kits). Then work through our [beginner soldering guide](/guides/how-to-solder-for-beginners).

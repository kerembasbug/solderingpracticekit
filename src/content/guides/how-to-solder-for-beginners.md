Soldering looks intimidating, but it is one of the most learnable skills in electronics. With the right tools and an hour of practice, you can make joints that are strong, shiny, and electrically sound. This guide walks you through everything from a cold start.

## What you'll need

Before your first joint, gather these essentials:

- **A soldering iron**, ideally a temperature-controlled station. Stable heat is the single biggest factor in good joints. A bench station like the [YIHUA 926 III](/reviews/yihua-926-iii-soldering-station) is an excellent first tool because it bundles tips and accessories.
- **Solder** — thin (0.6–0.8 mm) rosin-core solder is easiest to learn with. Leaded solder is the most forgiving; lead-free is required in some regions and runs a little hotter.
- **A practice kit** so you have something safe and rewarding to solder. The [amomii Testudo](/reviews/amomii-testudo-soldering-practice-kit) and the classic [Elenco SP-1A](/reviews/elenco-practical-soldering-project-kit) are our top beginner picks.
- **A brass-wool tip cleaner** (better than a wet sponge — it doesn't shock-cool the tip).
- **Helping hands or a small vise**, and **safety glasses** — solder can spit.
- Good ventilation. Never breathe the smoke; that's flux vapour.

## Step 1: Set up and stay safe

Work in a ventilated space on a heat-resistant surface. Put your iron in its stand, never on the bench. Tie back long hair, wear safety glasses, and keep a bottle of water nearby. The tip reaches 300°C+ — treat it with respect and assume it's always hot.

## Step 2: Tin the tip

"Tinning" means coating the tip with a thin layer of solder. It protects the tip and helps heat flow into the joint.

1. Bring the iron up to temperature (around 320°C / 610°F for leaded solder).
2. Wipe the tip on the brass wool.
3. Touch a little solder to the tip until it's bright and shiny.

A shiny, silver tip transfers heat well. A dull, blackened tip does not — re-tin it whenever it looks dull.

## Step 3: Make the joint

This is the technique that matters most. **Heat the joint, then feed the solder into the joint — not onto the iron.**

1. Press the tip against *both* the component lead and the copper pad at the same time.
2. Count "one, two," letting them heat up.
3. Feed solder into the point where the tip meets the pad. It should flow and wick around the joint.
4. Remove the solder, then remove the iron. The whole thing takes 2–3 seconds.

A good joint is shiny and looks like a small volcano or cone — concave sides, hugging the lead and pad. A bad ("cold") joint is dull, blobby, or ball-shaped because the metal never properly melted together.

## Step 4: Inspect and clean up

Look closely (a magnifier helps). The joint should be smooth and shiny with no gaps. Snip excess lead length with flush cutters. Wipe your tip and re-tin it before setting the iron down.

## Common beginner mistakes

- **Heating the solder, not the joint.** Always heat the metal you're joining.
- **Too little heat, or too slow.** A cold joint is brittle and unreliable. If solder beads up instead of flowing, add heat and flux.
- **Too much solder.** You want enough to fill the joint, not a giant blob.
- **A dirty tip.** Clean and tin often.
- **Moving the joint while it cools.** Hold steady for a second after removing the iron.

## Practice, practice, practice

There is no substitute for repetition. Start with a [practice kit](/best-soldering-practice-kits) that has plenty of through-hole joints, build something that works, then move on to [surface-mount practice](/guides/through-hole-vs-surface-mount-soldering) once your hands are steady. Within a few builds, clean joints will feel automatic.

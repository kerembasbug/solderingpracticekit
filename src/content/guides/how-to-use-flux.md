Ask an experienced solderer for their single best tip and a surprising number will say the same word: **flux**. It is the difference between solder that beads up and refuses to stick and solder that flows into a joint like water finding its level. Here is what flux actually does and how to use it.

## What flux does

When metal heats up in air it forms a thin layer of oxide, and solder will not bond to oxide. Flux is a mild chemical cleaner that, when heated, strips that oxide away and stops it reforming for the few seconds you need. It also lowers the surface tension of molten solder so it *wets* the joint — spreading into a smooth, shiny fillet instead of sitting in a dull ball.

Every good solder joint relies on flux. The only question is whether the flux already inside your solder is enough, or whether you need to add more.

## The types of flux

- **Rosin / RMA flux** — the classic. Made from pine resin, activated by heat, excellent for electronics. Leaves a sticky amber residue that is non-corrosive but worth cleaning with isopropyl alcohol for a tidy finish.
- **No-clean flux** — leaves a minimal, safe residue you can usually leave in place. The most convenient choice for hobby work and the most common in flux pens and modern pastes.
- **Water-soluble flux** — the most aggressive and best at cleaning heavily oxidised metal, but the residue is corrosive and **must** be washed off completely with water.

## How to use flux paste and pens

1. **Apply a small amount to the joint** — a dab of paste on the pad and component leg, or a quick swipe of a flux pen. You need very little.
2. **Heat the joint, not the flux.** Bring your iron onto the pad and leg as normal. The flux will bubble and activate as the metal reaches temperature.
3. **Feed solder into the joint.** With the oxide cleared, the solder will flow in and wet both surfaces, forming a clean, concave fillet.
4. **Clean up if needed.** Wipe rosin or water-soluble residue away with isopropyl alcohol; no-clean residue can usually stay.

A clean tip and the right temperature matter too — work at around 315–340°C (600–650°F) with leaded solder, and keep your tip [clean and tinned](/guides/how-to-clean-and-tin-a-soldering-iron-tip).

## When extra flux makes the biggest difference

- **Desoldering and rework** — flux makes old solder melt and flow again so it lifts cleanly with [a solder sucker](/reviews/viralloy-solder-sucker) or wick.
- **Surface-mount (SMD)** — fine-pitch parts need far more flux than solder wire carries; a pen or paste is essential.
- **Oxidised boards or legs** — old or cheap components often have dull, oxidised leads that solder refuses to wet until you add flux.

## Common mistakes

Beginners either skip flux entirely — and fight joints that will not take — or drown the board in it. Use a little, let it do its job, and clean up active residues. Pair good flux habits with the [right solder](/guides/lead-free-vs-leaded-solder) and you will be amazed how much easier soldering becomes.

The iron is the most important tool you'll buy, and the right one removes most of the frustration beginners feel. Here's how to choose without overspending.

## Iron vs. station

A plain **soldering iron** plugs into the wall and heats to a fixed temperature. A **soldering station** adds a base unit with electronic temperature control and a display.

For learning, a **station wins**. Stable, adjustable heat is the biggest single factor in clean joints, and stations usually come with a proper stand and tip selection. Our top pick, the [YIHUA 926 III](/reviews/yihua-926-iii-soldering-station), bundles helping hands and consumables too.

A bare iron like the [Q-MING 60W kit](/reviews/q-ming-60w-soldering-iron-kit) is fine as a low-cost way to test the hobby — just expect less precise temperature.

## Wattage and temperature

Don't confuse the two:

- **Wattage** is how much power the iron can draw. More watts = faster heat recovery after each joint. For electronics, **40–60W** is ideal.
- **Temperature** is how hot the tip actually gets. A temperature-controlled iron lets you set this — typically **315–340°C (600–650°F)** for leaded solder, a little higher for lead-free.

A 60W *temperature-controlled* iron is not more dangerous than a 30W one — it just recovers heat faster.

## Corded or cordless?

A **corded** station is best for a bench: unlimited runtime, stable heat, ideal for learning. A **cordless** iron like the [Fanttik T1 Max](/reviews/fanttik-t1-max-cordless-soldering-iron) is brilliant for portability and quick repairs but limited by battery life. Many makers eventually own both.

## Tips matter

The tip transfers heat to the joint, so its shape and condition are crucial:

- **Chisel / bevel** — versatile, great heat transfer; the best all-rounder.
- **Conical** — fine point for detailed and surface-mount work.
- Keep tips **clean and tinned**. A dull tip won't transfer heat no matter how good the iron.

## Don't forget the extras

Budget for: thin rosin-core solder, a brass-wool tip cleaner, flux, helping hands or a vise, a solder sucker or desoldering braid, and safety glasses. Some station kits include several of these, which is why bundles can be great value.

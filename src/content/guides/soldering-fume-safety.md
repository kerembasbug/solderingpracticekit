The wisp of smoke that curls up when solder melts is so routine that most beginners never think about it. They should — not because it is acutely dangerous, but because it is the one soldering hazard that is genuinely easy to ignore and genuinely worth managing.

## What the smoke actually is

Here is the key fact: **the smoke from soldering is vaporised flux, not lead.** Lead melts at around 327°C but does not boil or vaporise until far higher temperatures than any soldering iron reaches. So the white plume you see is the rosin flux — the stuff inside your [rosin-core solder](/reviews/hgmzzq-60-40-rosin-core-solder) — burning off as it does its job.

That changes how you protect yourself. The airborne risk is the flux smoke; the lead risk is from *handling* leaded solder and then touching your mouth, which is why hand-washing matters (more on that below).

## The health risk

Rosin flux fume is a recognised respiratory sensitiser. Occasional exposure in a ventilated room is low-risk, but **regular, close-up inhalation** can cause:

- Eye, nose and throat irritation
- Coughing and respiratory discomfort
- With heavy long-term exposure, occupational asthma

The dose makes the poison. A few minutes a month near an open window is very different from hours every day hunched over a board with the smoke drifting straight up into your face.

## How to protect yourself

**1. Keep the smoke out of your face.** This is the simplest, highest-impact step. A small carbon-filter [fume extractor](/reviews/kotto-solder-fume-extractor) sat beside your work pulls the plume sideways and away from you. It is the cheapest meaningful safety upgrade you can make.

**2. Ventilate.** Even an open window makes a real difference. For frequent soldering, the gold standard is a fume extractor that vents *outside* rather than just filtering and recirculating.

**3. Do not lean directly over the joint.** Position yourself so the rising smoke does not pass your nose. A little distance and a slight angle go a long way.

**4. Handle leaded solder responsibly.** Although the fumes are essentially lead-free, the solder itself is not. Do not eat or drink at the bench, and **wash your hands** when you finish. Better still, use [lead-free solder](/guides/lead-free-vs-leaded-solder) for work that will be handled by others or by children.

## A sensible setup

You do not need a lab. A [decent station](/reviews/yihua-926-iii-soldering-station) or iron, a small fume absorber beside your work, a window cracked open, and clean hands afterward covers the vast majority of hobby soldering safely. Add safety glasses when clipping component legs and you have a bench you can use for years without worry.

Walk into the solder aisle and the choices multiply fast: tin-lead ratios, diameters, flux cores, lead-free alloys. For electronics, though, the decision is simpler than it looks. Here is everything you actually need to know to buy the right spool.

## Leaded vs lead-free

The first fork in the road. **Leaded** solder — usually 60/40 or 63/37 tin-lead — melts at a lower temperature (around 183–190°C) and flows beautifully, which makes it the easiest to learn on. **Lead-free** solder (typically SAC alloys with tin, silver and copper) melts hotter (~217°C) and is stickier, but it is required for anything sold commercially or handled by children.

Our full breakdown lives in the [lead-free vs leaded guide](/guides/lead-free-vs-leaded-solder); the short version is **learn on leaded, switch to lead-free when you need to.** A spool of [60/40 rosin-core wire](/reviews/hgmzzq-60-40-rosin-core-solder) is the ideal first purchase.

## 60/40 vs 63/37

Among leaded solders you will see two ratios:

- **60/40** has a small "plastic range" — a few degrees where it is half-set as it cools. Disturb a joint in that window and you can get a dull, weak [cold joint](/guides/how-to-fix-a-cold-solder-joint).
- **63/37** is *eutectic*: it changes straight from liquid to solid with no pasty phase. That makes it slightly more forgiving and the choice of many professionals.

Both are great. If you can find 63/37 easily, take it; if not, 60/40 is perfectly good.

## Rosin core: why it matters

Almost all electronics solder is **rosin-core** (also called flux-core): the wire is hollow with flux running through the centre. As the solder melts, the flux is released exactly where it is needed to clean the joint. Never use acid-core or plumbing solder on electronics — the acid flux corrodes copper and ruins boards. For more on flux, see our [how to use flux guide](/guides/how-to-use-flux).

## Choosing a diameter (gauge)

Thinner wire gives you finer control over how much solder you feed; thicker wire delivers more, faster.

| Diameter | Best for |
|----------|----------|
| **0.6 mm** | Fine, dense or surface-mount work |
| **0.8 mm** | The all-rounder — most through-hole electronics |
| **1.0 mm+** | Large joints: speaker tabs, thick wires, ground planes |

If you buy one spool, make it **0.8 mm** — it handles the vast majority of hobby joints comfortably.

## How much to buy

A 50–100 g spool lasts a beginner a long time. Buy a small spool of leaded 0.8 mm to learn on, and add a thinner 0.6 mm spool and a lead-free spool later as your projects demand.
